    Ideation

    The idea behind my project was inspired by the motion sensor lights used in school toilets. Motion lights are very useful as they increase convenience for us. We don't have to deal with switches to turn the lights on or off. All we need to do is walk in range of the senso, and the light will automatically turn on. Similarly, the light will turn off once we are out of the sensor range. Here is the initial sketch I made for the project. As you can see, while the concept remained more or less the same, the design went through some changes:








    Initially, I was thinking of using lightbulbs which required a relay to control the voltage, however, I ended up switching to neopixels as they were more economical. I also shifted the sensor to the bottom surface of the box and angled it at 45degrees so that it can detect people that are walking towards the light. I was also forced to modify the top cover to include a lid so that I could easily access the circuit for maintainence and make any last minute changes if I needed to make them. To



    Designing Process

    Once I had a rough idea of what I wanted to do with my motion lights, I designed the circuit in TinkerCAD and tested it to make sure that it would actually function

    My code is also in the TinkerCAD if you wish to take a look at my code

    The live demonstration of my basic circuit will be included in a small video at the end of this page

    Once I had built my circuit, I started designing the items that I was going to cut and 3D print to make my box

    First up was the box body itself, designed using the ideas that I mentioned in the ideation stage

    As can be seen in the design, the box is intended to represent a false ceiling or it can be ceiling mounted in box form. The sensor is placed on the tilted surface on the bottom to detect a person that is approaching the device. The ceiling mounted/false ceiling was inspired by my bathroom lights. 2 of my bathroom lights are in a false celing while for the central light, the cover is held in place using hooks

    Here are some images for refrence

    False Ceiling Lights

    Once I designed the box, I designed a holder to hold the PIR sensor so that it wouldn't fall from the angled board and become misaligned

    Initially, I used a holder with slender, cylinder shaped legs to hold the sensor (as can be seen in the 3D model), however, as the design was not very feasible, I was forced to change the holder design to a table-like shape as can be seen below:






    The final part I designed was the "lampshade". The purpose of this "lampshade" was to provide me with a proper surface that I could line with a reflective material such as aluminium foil. The relective material would reflect the light rays and amplify the amount of light emitted by the device.





    Because the overall size of the "lampshade" was too large for the 3D printer, I split the lampshade into symmetrical halves and printed them at the same time. The reason I decided to go for 3D printing over laser cutting was because of the jutting out surface at the top. It would be easier to 3D print that kind of shape, as compared to lasercutting anf sticking the wood pieces together. It would also save space as I only needed to 3D print a thickness of 2mm, while the thinnest sheet of plywood is 3.5mm which is too thick

    Once the design was done, all I had to do was create the functions to program the 3D printer and laser cutter
